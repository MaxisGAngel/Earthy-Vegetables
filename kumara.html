How to grow Kumara

A Step by step Guide

Kumara (sweet potato) is a nutritious and versatile root vegetable that thrives in warm climates. With proper care, kumara can produce a bountiful harvest in about 4-5 months. Follow this guide to successfully grow your own kumara.

Difficulty Rating: Moderate
Best Season to Grow: Spring and Summer
Can Be Grown: Outdoors

Supplies Needed to Grow Kumara

  • Kumara slips (sprouted shoots from a mature kumara)

  • Well-draining, sandy or loamy soil

  • Compost or balanced fertilizer (low in nitrogen)

  • Garden fork or tiller

  • Mulch (straw, leaves, or grass clippings)

  • Raised garden bed (optional)

  • Watering can or hose

Setting Up Your Kumara Garden

Choosing the Right Location

  • Sunlight: Kumara thrives in full sun, requiring at least 6-8 hours of direct sunlight per day.

  • Soil: Use light, well-draining soil with a pH between 5.5 and 6.5.

  • Spacing: Plant slips 12-18 inches apart in rows spaced 3-4 feet apart to allow proper vine growth.

  • Raised Beds or Mounds: Improve drainage by planting kumara in raised beds or soil mounds.


Preparing the Soil

  • Loosen the soil to a depth of at least 12 inches for proper root development.

  • Mix compost or well-rotted manure into the soil to improve fertility.

  • Avoid using high-nitrogen fertilizers, as they promote excessive vine growth at the expense of tubers.


Planting Kumara

  • Growing Slips: Place a mature kumara in water or damp soil to encourage sprouting. Once sprouts reach 6-8 inches, gently twist them off and plant them.

  • Planting Slips: Insert slips deep enough to cover the lower leaves, leaving only the top leaves exposed.

  • Water Immediately: After planting, water thoroughly to help the slips establish.


Caring for Your Kumara Plants

Watering

  • Keep the soil evenly moist but not waterlogged, especially during the first few weeks.

  • Reduce watering as the vines establish, only watering when the soil is dry.

Fertilizing

  • Apply a low-nitrogen fertilizer or compost once a month to encourage tuber growth.

  • Avoid excessive feeding, as too much nitrogen can reduce root production.

Mulching & Weed Control

  • Apply mulch to retain moisture, regulate soil temperature, and suppress weeds.

  • Hand-pull weeds carefully to avoid disturbing the shallow kumara roots.

Pest and Disease Prevention

  • Watch for pests such as wireworms, aphids, and caterpillars.

  • Use organic pest control methods like neem oil or companion planting.

  • Ensure proper drainage to prevent fungal diseases like root rot.

Harvesting

  • Kumara is ready to harvest in 4-5 months, when the leaves start turning yellow.

  • Gently loosen the soil and carefully dig up the tubers without damaging them.

  • Cure the harvested kumara by drying them in a warm, airy place for 7-10 days before storage.
